Section 4.2 Section 4.2

Activity 4.2.1.

  1. Suppose that \(A\) is a square matrix and that the nonzero vector \(\xvec\) is a solution to the homogeneous equation \(A\xvec = \zerovec\text{.}\) What can we conclude about the invertibility of \(A\text{?}\)

    1. \(A\) is invertible.

    2. \(A\) is not invertible.

    3. \(A\) could be either invertible or not invertible.

  2. How does the determinant \(\det A\) tell us if there is a nonzero solution to the homogeneous equation \(A\xvec = \zerovec\text{?}\)

    1. The determinant of \(A\) must be 0.

    2. The determinant of \(A\) must be nonzero.

    3. The determinant of \(A\) must be 1.

Activity 4.2.2.

  1. Suppose that

    \begin{equation*} A = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 3 \amp -1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 4 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Find the determinant \(\det A\text{.}\) What does this tell us about the solution space to the homogeneous equation \(A\xvec = \zerovec\text{?}\)

  2. Find a basis for \(\nul(A)\text{.}\)

  3. What is the relationship between the rank of a matrix and the dimension of its null space?

Activity 4.2.3.

The eigenvalues of a square matrix are defined by the condition that there be a nonzero solution to the homogeneous equation \((A-\lambda I)\vvec=\zerovec\text{.}\)

  1. If there is a nonzero solution to the homogeneous equation \((A-\lambda I)\vvec = \zerovec\text{,}\) what can we conclude about the invertibility of the matrix \(A-\lambda I\text{?}\)

    1. \(A-\lambda I\) is invertible.

    2. \(A-\lambda I\) is not invertible.

    3. \(A-\lambda I\) could be either invertible or not invertible, depending on the invertibility of \(A\text{.}\)

  2. If there is a nonzero solution to the homogeneous equation \((A-\lambda I)\vvec = \zerovec\text{,}\) what can we conclude about the determinant \(\det(A-\lambda I)\text{?}\)

    1. The determinant of \(A-\lambda I\) must be 0.

    2. The determinant of \(A-\lambda I\) must be nonzero.

    3. The determinant of \(A\) must equal the determinant of \(\lambda I\text{.}\)

Activity 4.2.4.

  1. Let's consider the matrix

    \begin{equation*} A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}

    from which we construct

    \begin{equation*} A-\lambda I = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] - \lambda \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1-\lambda \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1-\lambda \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Find the determinant \(\det(A-\lambda I)\text{.}\)

  2. What kind of equation do you obtain when we set this determinant to zero to obtain \(\det(A-\lambda I) = 0\text{?}\)

  3. Use the determinant you found in the previous part to find the eigenvalues \(\lambda\) by solving \(\det(A-\lambda I) = 0\text{.}\) We considered this matrix in the previous section so we should find the same eigenvalues for \(A\) that we found by reasoning geometrically there.

Activity 4.2.5.

  1. Consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \\ \end{array}\right]\) and find its eigenvalues by solving the equation \(\det(A-\lambda I) = 0\text{.}\)

  2. Consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 0 \amp -1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \\ \end{array}\right]\) and find its eigenvalues by solving the equation \(\det(A-\lambda I) = 0\text{.}\)

Activity 4.2.6.

  1. Find the eigenvalues of the triangular matrix \(\left[\begin{array}{rrr} 3 \amp -1 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \text{.}\)

  2. What is generally true about the eigenvalues of a triangular matrix?

In the next few activities, we will find the eigenvectors of a matrix as the null space of the matrix \(A-\lambda I\text{.}\)

Activity 4.2.7.

  1. Let's begin with the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] \text{.}\) We have seen that \(\lambda = 3\) is an eigenvalue. Form the matrix \(A-3I\) and find a basis for the eigenspace \(E_3 = \nul(A-3I)\text{.}\)

  2. What is the dimension of this eigenspace?

  3. For each of the basis vectors \(\vvec\text{,}\) verify that \(A\vvec = 3\vvec\text{.}\)

  4. We also saw that \(\lambda = -1\) is an eigenvalue. Form the matrix \(A-(-1)I\) and find a basis for the eigenspace \(E_{-1}\text{.}\)

  5. What is the dimension of this eigenspace?

  6. For each of the basis vectors \(\vvec\text{,}\) verify that \(A\vvec = -\vvec\text{.}\)

  7. Is it possible to form a basis of \(\real^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{?}\)

Activity 4.2.8.

  1. Now consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 3 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 3 \\ \end{array}\right] \text{.}\) Write the characteristic equation for \(A\) and use it to find the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)

  2. For each eigenvalue, find a basis for its eigenspace \(E_\lambda\text{.}\)

  3. Is it possible to form a basis of \(\real^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{?}\)

Activity 4.2.9.

  1. Next, consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \\ \end{array}\right] \text{.}\) Write the characteristic equation for \(A\) and use it to find the eigenvalues of \(A\text{.}\)

  2. For each eigenvalue, find a basis for its eigenspace \(E_\lambda\text{.}\)

  3. Is it possible to form a basis of \(\real^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{?}\)

Activity 4.2.10.

  1. Finally, find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the diagonal matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 4 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \\ \end{array}\right] \text{.}\)

  2. Explain your result by considering the geometric effect of the matrix transformation defined by \(A\text{.}\)

Once we find the eigenvalues of a matrix \(A\text{,}\) describing the eigenspace \(E_\lambda\) amounts to the familiar task of describing the null space \(\nul(A-\lambda I)\text{.}\)

Activity 4.2.11.

  1. Identify the eigenvalues, and their multiplicities, of an \(n\times n\) matrix whose characteristic polynomial is \((2-\lambda)^3(-3-\lambda)^{10}(5-\lambda)\text{.}\)

  2. What can you conclude about the dimensions of the eigenspaces?

  3. What is the dimension of the matrix?

  4. Do you have enough information to guarantee that there is a basis of \(\real^n\) consisting of eigenvectors?

Activity 4.2.12.

  1. Find the eigenvalues of \(\left[\begin{array}{rr} 0 \amp -1 \\ 4 \amp -4 \\ \end{array}\right]\) and state their multiplicities.

  2. Can you find a basis of \(\real^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of this matrix?

Activity 4.2.13.

Consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -1 \amp 0 \amp 2 \\ -2 \amp -2 \amp -4 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -2 \\ \end{array}\right]\) whose characteristic equation is

\begin{equation*} (-2-\lambda)^2(-1-\lambda) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

  1. Identify the eigenvalues and their multiplicities.

  2. For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) find a basis of the eigenspace \(E_\lambda\) and state its dimension.

  3. Is there a basis of \(\real^3\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{?}\)

Activity 4.2.14.

Now consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -5 \amp -2 \amp -6 \\ -2 \amp -2 \amp -4 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ \end{array}\right]\) whose characteristic equation is also

\begin{equation*} (-2-\lambda)^2(-1-\lambda) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

  1. Identify the eigenvalues and their multiplicities.

  2. For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) find a basis of the eigenspace \(E_\lambda\) and state its dimension.

  3. Is there a basis of \(\real^3\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{?}\)

Activity 4.2.15.

Consider the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} -5 \amp -2 \amp -6 \\ 4 \amp 1 \amp 8 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ \end{array}\right]\) whose characteristic equation is

\begin{equation*} (-2-\lambda)(1-\lambda)(-1-\lambda) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

  1. Identify the eigenvalues and their multiplicities.

  2. For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) find a basis of the eigenspace \(E_\lambda\) and state its dimension.

  3. Is there a basis of \(\real^3\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\text{?}\)
